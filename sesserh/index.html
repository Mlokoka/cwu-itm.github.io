Seeing the Bigger Picture: Systems Thinking and MBSE in Action

Modern problems are rarely isolated. When designing software, managing infrastructure, or building global systems. We a are increasingly working in interconnected environments. We have begun exploring how systems thinking helps us better understand and manage complexity. This post reflects on that concept, its differences from traditional thinking, and how a model-based approach like MBSE (Model-Based Systems Engineering) helps us better navigate the digital world.

What is Systems Thinking?

Systems thinking is a way of understanding problems by looking at the whole system, not just a singular individual part. Rather than focusing only on the immediate issue, it encourages us to explore how different components interact and influence one another over time. According to Holt (2021), systems thinking requires shifting our perspective from “what is broken” to “how do the parts interact to create this result?” This change in mindset helps engineers and problem-solvers recognize hidden feedback loops, unintended consequences, and long-term patterns.

For example, imagine a university IT helpdesk struggling with slow ticket response times. A traditional fix might involve hiring more staff. Systems thinking, however might uncover a deeper issue that students are receiving inadequate technical training which in turn leads to more support requests. Or perhaps even outdated tools cause delays across multiple departments. Systems thinking looks at the entire network of causes and relationships instead of treating symptoms.

Linear Thinking vs. Systems Thinking

Traditional also known as linear thinking typically focuses on one-directional cause and effect scenarios such as “A causes B.” It works well for straightforward problems but falls short when complexity enters the equation. In contrast, systems thinking embraces circular cause-and-effect and recognizies that “A influences B, which may also influence A.” This feedback loop concept is critical in systems engineering.

Holt (2021) contrasts these two mindsets by showing that linear thinking leads to reactive decision-making, while systems thinking enables proactive design. For instance, in digital systems, a linear approach might optimize a single component like speed, which could unintentionally make other parts unstable. Systems thinking helps anticipate those ripple effects.

This shift from breaking problems into parts to viewing the entire system is what makes systems thinking so relevant in today’s interconnected environments. According to the International Council on Systems Engineering, systems engineering depends on understanding lifecycle impacts and how interconnected elements influence one another across complex architectures (INCOSE, 2015).

Why Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) Matters

As complexity grows, engineers need more than intuition to manage it they need a clear visual model of how everything fits together. That’s where MBSE comes in. MBSE replaces traditional document-based engineering with digital models that represent systems and their relationships. These models allow engineers to visualize how parts interact, simulate outcomes, and ensure that the design stays aligned with the system’s goals throughout the lifecycle.

For instance, when designing a smart home ecosystem, MBSE allows you to model devices, software, user interactions, and data flows. Instead of building the system first and fixing problems later, MBSE helps teams build a blueprint beforehand identifying potential bottlenecks and inefficiencies early.

Personal Reflection

Before IT 248, I often approached problems by jumping straight into solutions. But systems thinking has challenged me to take a step back and consider the broader environment. I’ve learned that sometimes, what looks like a technical failure is actually a process issue or even a cultural one. For example, in a past group project, our slow progress wasn’t because of a bad workflow, but because team roles were unclear. A systems mindset would have helped me identify that sooner.

What surprised me most is how useful systems thinking is outside of engineering. Whether managing a team, solving a customer service problem, or even planning personal goals, thinking in terms of relationships and patterns can lead to better decisions. Meadows (2008) notes that systems thinking gives us the ability to find leverage points — places in a system where a small shift can produce big change.

Conclusion

Systems thinking gives us the tools to tackle complexity by shifting our focus from isolated parts to interacting systems. It’s a mindset that helps uncover the root causes of problems and anticipate the consequences of decisions. When combined with Model-Based Systems Engineering, this approach becomes even more powerful — allowing engineers to create clear, adaptable models of the systems they’re building. As we’ve explored in IT 248, this integrated perspective isn’t just useful — it’s essential in the design and management of complex digital environments.

Understanding Life Cycle Thinking in Systems Engineering

In systems engineering, the goal is not just to build something and be done. Systems are dynamic and go through a full journey. This journey is called the systems development life cycle, which guides a system from concept to retirement. As explained in Systems Engineering Demystified (Holt, 2023), it helps engineers plan for every phase of a system’s life, not just the design stage.

Thinking in terms of a life cycle allows teams to consider the long-term impacts of their decisions. It promotes smarter planning, reduces risk, and helps make systems that are sustainable, adaptable, and easier to manage throughout their use.

Life Cycle Models in Systems Engineering

There are several ways to approach the development of a system. The three most common models are the linear model, the iterative model, and the incremental model. Each offers its own advantages and drawbacks, depending on the goals and flexibility of the project.

Linear Model

The linear model, sometimes called the waterfall model, moves through each phase in order. You start with requirements, move to design, then build, test, and maintain.

  • Easy to manage with clear steps
  • Works well when requirements will not change
  • Hard to go back if issues are found later

This model works best in environments where everything needs to be locked in from the start. It is predictable, but not very flexible.

Iterative Model

The iterative model focuses on repeating the development cycle. Each cycle produces a working version that improves based on feedback.

  • Encourages learning as you build
  • Responds better to changing requirements
  • Can waste time without clear direction

This is common in software development and is useful when the end goal is evolving. It supports refinement and exploration, which makes it ideal for innovative work (Estefan, 2007).

Incremental Model

The incremental model delivers the system in pieces. Each piece adds functionality until the full system is complete.

  • Delivers working parts faster
  • Supports shifting priorities
  • Can create integration challenges

This approach helps users see progress early and give feedback. It balances structure with flexibility and is often used in product rollouts and digital platforms (Walden et al., 2015).

Why Systems Life Cycle Thinking Matters

Thinking across the entire life of a system helps avoid surprises. For example, some large system failures happened not because of bad design, but because the long-term use was not considered. Life cycle thinking helps engineers ask: how will this system be maintained, updated, or retired?

This thinking also improves collaboration. Everyone on the team sees the big picture and knows how their part fits in. Combined with model-based systems engineering (MBSE), this makes the whole development process more transparent and effective (Holt, 2023).

MBSE and the Future of Systems Development

MBSE is changing how we build systems. Instead of relying only on documents, engineers use models to manage complexity. These models show how parts connect, how behavior changes, and how everything fits together.

MBSE tools help teams avoid errors and spot issues early. This is especially important in large systems where changes in one area can affect the whole. MBSE supports life cycle thinking by making information more visible and easier to manage across all phases (Estefan, 2007).

Final Thoughts

Systems engineering is not just about solving today’s problems. It is about planning for tomorrow. Life cycle models give structure, while MBSE helps teams stay connected. Together, they help engineers build systems that are useful, sustainable, and ready for whatever comes next.

Designing for People: Perception, Empathy, and the Power of Human-Centered Design

Design is not just about function. It is about people. Whether creating a digital interface, a service, or a product, human-centered design begins by understanding how people perceive, think, and feel. When we design for people, we focus on empathy, observation, and how humans interact with technology. Principles like perception, attention, and cognitive limits are essential.

What is Human-Centered Design?

Human-centered design is a way of solving problems that begins with the people who will use the solution. According to the ISO 9241-210 standard, human-centered design is an approach to interactive systems development that aims to make systems usable and useful by focusing on the users, their needs and requirements (International Organization for Standardization, 2019). The ISO model includes clear steps like understanding the context, setting user needs, and testing solutions.

The IDEO Field Guide offers a more flexible approach. It supports open thinking, observation, and creative idea generation. Where ISO gives structure, IDEO focuses on storytelling and empathy. Both methods keep users at the center and value real human insight.

Perception and Visual Hierarchy in Usability

Human perception affects how we understand and use digital interfaces. A good layout guides attention. A bad one makes users feel confused. Visual hierarchy means organizing elements to show importance. Using size, contrast, and spacing to highlight buttons and headings helps users know where to go next.

Johnson (2020) explains that early choices like layout and button size can help or hurt usability. A site with equal text sizes for all content may hide important actions. People often rely on quick judgments. Design should support those quick moments of decision.

UX Laws That Guide Design

  • Jakob’s Law says people prefer websites that feel familiar. If most sites use a top-right menu, moving it can confuse users (Weinschenk, 2011).
  • Fitts’s Law shows that the closer and larger a button is, the faster users can click it. Making buttons easy to reach is especially helpful on mobile (Wickens et al., 2015).
  • Hick’s Law tells us that more choices take more time. Designers should limit options or show them gradually to keep users focused (Johnson, 2020).
  • Miller’s Law says people can only hold about seven items in memory. Grouping or chunking information makes it easier to understand (Miller, 1956).

Empathy and Early Design Choices

Design begins with empathy. Research like interviews and observation reveals problems users may not mention. For example, an audit might show that people with low vision struggle with color contrast. Fixing this early can lead to a more accessible and inclusive design (W3C, 2023).

Skipping early research often leads to designs that are hard to use. But when designers listen and observe from the start, they can create products that feel easy and natural. Both ISO and IDEO remind us that understanding people is the key to better design.

Conclusion

Designing for people means caring about how they think and feel. Whether using the structure of ISO 9241-210 or the creativity of IDEO, the goal is the same. Good design supports attention, avoids overload, and respects human limits. By applying UX laws and staying focused on real people, we can create tools that are helpful, clear, and human.
